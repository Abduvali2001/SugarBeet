Fertilizer

Sugar beets are very demanding on nutritional conditions. The soil on which beets are cultivated must contain sufficient reserves of various nutrients. The nutrients that beets use in their development in large quantities belong to the group of macronutrients - nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), potassium (K), sulphur (S), magnesium (Mg), calcium (Ca). Substances that beets use in smaller quantities to build their body (grams per 1 ton of mass) - to the group of trace elements: boron (B), manganese (Mn), zinc (Zn), copper (Cu), iron (Fe), molybdenum (Mo) et al.

Since the soils on which beets are grown do not contain enough easily digestible reserves of some nutrients, they need to be added additionally. To obtain high and guaranteed yields of root crops, mineral and organic fertilizers are used that contain the most important nutrients - nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium. Under irrigation conditions, a complete nitrogen-phosphorus-potassium fertilizer provides more than 50% of an increase in root yield. But the greatest impact on productivity is exerted by the combined use of a complete mineral fertilizer with organic (manure).

According to the nature of the absorption of nutrients in the growth and development of sugar beets, three periods are distinguished: germination and initial growth until true leaves are formed, enhanced leaf growth, and root growth and development.

In the first period, young plants are most in need of available nitrogen and phosphorus. At this time, the roots are still poorly developed, and in cold weather, the movement of nutrients is impeded. Therefore, the introduction of small doses of phosphorus (P15–20) and nitrogen (N10–15) during sowing is considered a must and has a positive effect on plant productivity.

In the second period, when there is an enhanced formation of the leaf apparatus, the beets need a large amount of nitrogen. During this period, plants consume more than 50% of all nitrogen required for the growing season. This, as well as the location of nitrate nitrogen in the lower soil layers (below 50 cm) in early spring, necessitates the introduction of nitrogen fertilizers in the early period of plant growth. This is especially important on light soils.

In the third period, all three basic nutrients are needed with a predominance of phosphorus and potassium.

For the best provision of sugar beet at different periods of its development, fertilizers should be applied in three doses: the main one - in the fall for ploughing, row - in the spring when sowing and in feeding during the initial growing season.

Nitrogen fertilizer can be applied in three steps: up to 70% of the annual dose - from autumn under plowing, 10-15 kg / ha in rows when sowing and the remaining amount - for top dressing. With a close occurrence of groundwater and the underlying pebble layer, nitrogen fertilizer is applied in rows of 10-15 kg / ha, and the remaining fertilizer is applied in two additional fertilizing.

The first of them should be done as early as possible, and the second before the first watering or 4 weeks after the first feeding. Top dressing later than June 15 should not be carried out, since late dressings reduce the technological qualities of root crops and plant immunity to diseases.

When using potassium fertilizers (chlorine containing), it must be borne in mind that on saline soils, they can cause an increase in the toxic effect of salts. This leads to a decrease in yield, so it is better to refuse to use them.

As for the doses of fertilizers, they are set depending on the type of soil and the level of soil fertility, for which it is necessary to periodically conduct a chemical analysis of the soil of the site. For example, on medium-abundant meadow- gray earth and gray earth meadow soils in old-ploughed fields (i.e., distant in the crop rotation from alfalfa for 3 years or more), the annual dose of nitrogen is 120 kg / ha, phosphorus - 150 kg / ha, potassium - 70 kg / ha. According to the seam turnover (i.e. one year after alfalfa), the annual dose of nitrogen, taking into account the nitrogen-fixing ability of alfalfa, is 90 kg / ha. On light and ordinary gray earth soils of the foothill zone, in which there is less humus than in gray earth-meadow soils, the nitrogen dose should be increased by 20–30%. When applying organic fertilizers, the dose of nitrogen fertilizers must be reduced by 20–30 kg / ha. When using straw as fertilizer, the nitrogen rate is increased at the rate of N10 for each ton of straw. Straw enhances the activity of microorganisms, which leads to temporary "nitrogen starvation".

The use of excessively high doses of nitrogen fertilizers causes a sharp decrease in the sugar content of beets and the production of low-quality raw materials for processing (amide nitrogen increases, which prevents the extraction of sugar). Excess phosphorus and even potash fertilizers also reduce sugar content, not providing an increase in yield. When applying organic fertilizers, the dose of phosphorus should be reduced by 40-50 kg / ha, and potassium by 30-40 kg / ha. This is because plants receive a significant amount of nutrients from organic fertilizers, including phosphorus and potassium.

The most common organic fertilizer is manure. It is better to apply it half-rotted, since fresh manure serves as a source of replenishment of weed seeds and dodders in the soil. The recommended dose of manure is 30–40 t / ha.

Chicken manure is a very valuable fast-acting organic fertilizer - in experiments, the application of 20 t / ha under the low increased the yield of roots. The greatest return on organic fertilizers is noted in the fields that are distant in the crop rotation from perennial grasses (i.e., old-ploughed fields).

Good results were obtained from the introduction of 5.4 t / ha of thermally dried manure (earlier it was produced by poultry farms under the name “powder”), which did not contain viable weed seeds. Productivity was higher than from the use of 30 t / ha manure by 5-9 t / ha.

When using vermicomposting (manure processed by earthworms) in a dose of 2–3 t / ha for pre-sowing cultivation, the yield of root crops increased and the incidence of rot in root crops decreased.

Plants use nutrients most effectively from fertilizers embedded in soil with stable moisture, that is, in a humid environment. For example, urea (urea), left on the soil surface without embedding loses up to 60% of ammonia during decomposition during the first 6–8 days after sieving.

The effectiveness of the fertilizer system is fully manifested with strict adherence to the recommendations and all other agro technical methods for the cultivation of sugar beets.

Sugar beet plants exhibit a reaction that can be recognized by a typical symptom when any nutrient is lacking. With a lack of nitrogen in plants, slow development is observed, they turn yellow and wither. Then old leaves fall off them, and the young ones remain small and narrow, with long petioles, erect. A sign of phosphorus deficiency is the edges of the leaves of a violet-reddish colour, dark green leaves, and the slowed down development of the plant. Lack of potassium leads to marginal leaf necrosis. In addition, in our conditions, there is a lack of the most important of the microelements - boron.

Our soils have an alkaline reaction, which contributes to the fixation of boron. Due to the lack of this trace element, young leaves at the point of growth may die off, and rot of the core may occur. When the first symptoms of boron deficiency appear, foliar feeding with boron-containing preparations is necessary. The content of soluble boron in the soil should be at least 1.1–2.0 mg / kg of soil. In recent years, in various regions of the Chui oblast, the symptoms of boron deficiency have been encountered more and more often, as a result of which the yield and quality of root crops decrease. Because of this, it is necessary to carry out foliar feeding with boron-containing preparations in the early phases of beet development (6–12 leaves) for prophylactic purposes. For this, you can use amino or, Ultramar boron 1–2 l / ha or other boron-containing preparations.

The active substance is the main chemical (nutrient) element contained in the fertilizer / fertilizer / in%.

For example, ammonium nitrate contains 34% of the active substance (nutrient) nitrogen. This means that 100 kg of ammonium nitrate contains 34 kg of nitrogen.

How to calculate the fertilizer dose? Suppose, according to soil analysis, it is recommended to apply per 1 hectare in kg a.c. N 120, P 150, K 70. Mineral fertilizers are available: ammonium nitrate, ammophos (Kazakhstan), PKU-phosphorus-potassium fertilizer (Russia). How much and what kind of fertilizer do you need to apply? We start the calculations with complex ammophos and the element with the highest phosphorus content. To add the recommended dose of phosphorus P150, we use the formula:

Y = D x 100 / C

Y is the fertilizer dose per hectare, kg; D is the dose of the active substance per hectare, kg; C is the content of the active substance in the fertilizer,%; U (ammophos) = 150 x 100/46 = 326 kg / ha

To provide plants with phosphorus per hectare, 326 kg of ammophos must be applied. Ammophos also contains 10% nitrogen. 326 x 10% = 32.6 kg. On the recommendation, the dose of nitrogen per hectare is 120 kg. It is necessary to bring 120 - 32.6 = 87.4 kg

U (ammonium nitrate) = 87.4 x 100/34 = 257 kg / ha

Let's calculate the potassium dose.

U (fku) = 70 x 100/55 = 127 kg / ha

PKU contains 5% phosphorus 127 x 5% = 6.3 kg. You can neglect this amount. As a result, in order to provide plants with nutrients, it is necessary to apply the following amount of fertilizers per hectare: ammonium nitrate - about 260 kg, ammophos - 320 kg, PKU - 130 kg.

To obtain an economically viable crop, it is necessary to apply basic fertilizers, then plant protection, and then the regulation and development of plant growth, foliar feeding, microelements, growth regulators, etc.
