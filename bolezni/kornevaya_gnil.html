Root rot

Press to see the photograph

Root rot can be one of the reasons for the decrease in yield and sugar content of sugar beets. Brown and fusarium rot are more common in the Chuy valley.

The first is characterized by decay of root crops and drying of leaves. Decay begins from the tail, then, expanding, it covers the entire root crop. Rot has a dark brown color and a sharp border from healthy tissue.

The causative agent of the disease is the mushroom Rhizoctonia solani. This pathogen is present in all soils and persists for up to 8 years or more; however, it is more often found in fields where beets are sown on beets continuously for several years in a row. It manifests itself more often on heavy floating unstructured soils, in places with a close occurrence of groundwater. It develops especially intensively with the onset of hot weather with abundant excessive watering and in areas where water is retained.

Fungi cause fusarium rot from the genus Fusarium. They penetrate into the root vegetable through damaged lateral roots, infect the vascular system of the root, cause at first the wilting of the leaves, and then the decay of the root, the tissues of which acquire a dark colour. The disease develops actively, usually in July-August during a drought.

There are also other types of rot - red rot, late blight, scab.
